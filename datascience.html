The Future of Astronomy

Leveraging observations from massive surveys including SDSS, DESI, WFIRST, and LSST

Software Development with the NOAO Data Lab

Beginning in early 2019, DESI will collect 30+ million spectra of galaxies and quasars in order to explore the influence of dark energy on the expansion of the universe. As host of public DESI data releases, the NOAO Data Lab seeks to develop open-access software tools for efficient spectral analysis. After discerning the most valuable spectral visualization functions, I will create them for use within a Jupyter Notebook, thus optimizing their accessibility among the greater astronomical community.

La Serena School for Data Science

The La Serena School for Data Science is a 10-day program in which students participate in intensive lectures on machine learning and statistical analysis, advanced photometric visualization tools, and high-performance computing. Students then apply these skills in collaborative projects that address real-world questions within astronomy, biology, and economics. While at the School, my project involved training a machine learning algorithm to automatically classify light curves from variable stars. My group members first queried time series data from the Optical Gravitational Lensing Experiment (OGLE) and determined their features using the Feature Analysis for Time Series. I then explored clustering, visualization, and classification methods with which to analyze our data and train our classifier (K-means, t-SNE, random forest). Because of this experience, I am now more equipped to apply these techniques within my own research and effectively share those skills with others. GitHub: lfulmer.

Undergraduate Course: Data Science

At the University of Wisconsin-Madison, I completed a course that outlined the general methodology of a data science workflow (data acquisition, data wrangling, knowledge inference) and applied those skills in the analysis of product rating statistics. Within a group project, I investigated trends in beer popularity by extracting brewery, style, and ABV information from online beer rating databases (Python: Scrapy). After performing quality assurance measures on the resulting data sets, we studied systematic trends between these three properties and the final product ratings offered by beer connoisseurs. We found that ABV consistently held as a good predictor of final rating, and that beers from Michigan and California were most popular among connoisseurs. Coming from the Land of Beer and Cheese, this was very disappointing.
